Undergraduate Research on the Olin Living Lab

Project Co-Manager | Spring 2013 - Current

The Olin Living Lab project is a student-driven effort to design, model, and construct a self-sustaining building on Olin’s campus. This building will be essentially off the grid; it will collect and treat its own water and generate its own electricity. In a focused team of two, I am currently researching the best approach to creating a thermal design strategy for net zero high glazing buildings in temperate climates. This research includes designing Revit building configurations, determing heating and cooling loads of those configurations, and performing COMSOL pipe flow analysis on different high- and low- temperature heating and cooling systems for eventual implimentation in HVAC design in future student-built, LBC-certified building space at Olin.

Throughout my extensive work on this project, I have gained valuable skills using industry-standard building design and performance analysis software (Autodesk Revit, Green Building Studio, Vasari, eQuest), pipeflow and heat transfer simulation software (COMSOL), and have experienced first-hand some of the intricacies of green building design and specifically the Living Building Challenge.

With this project, my partner and I have been awarded grants by Clare Boothe Luce Research Scholars Program, acquired Autodesk Building Performance Analysis Certification, and have presented among fellow undergraduates at regional poster sessions and doctorates and the heads of the industry in an international sustainability conference held in Copenhagen, Denmark called OnSustainability.

Mechanical Cassava Press for Use by Small-Scale Gari-Producing Women in Ghana

Affordable Design and Entrepreneurship | C-Frame Press Design Lead | Fall 2014

The ADE Ghana team has been developing electrical cassava graters and mechanical presses for 2 years to improve the livelihoods of small-scale gari-producing women in Ghana. Currently, these women suffer from an immense amount of physical burden in order to make a living through gari production, as their current tools consist of, essentially, an over-sized cheese grater and several 50-pound rocks to grate and press their cassava crop. With the current state of gari production a standard 80lb bag of gari takes several days of back-breaking work to complete.

Several semesters ago, the ADE Firm and the Ghana Team recognized this as an opportunity to both help tens of thousands of women across Ghana, and also create a venture borne from Olin. Two years later, the electrical cassava grater is in its final stage of development and is ready for deployment in Ghana. The cassava press is not far behind, as we are currently working to create the most effective and affordable design for sale to these women.

As the technical lead on the C-frame press design, I am developing, manufacturing, and co-designing this gari press for and with these women, for deployment and initial testing in Ghana in May 2015. My work on this project includes press design ideation and conceptualization, interaction sketch model creation, mechanical design of all interfaces of the press, and building, testing and iterating through a first pass prototype.

The Gauntlet

User Oriented Collaborative Design | Spring 2014

User-Oriented Collaborative Design, or UOCD, is the third class in the design curriculum series at Olin. This class, though it does not ask us to create a functional prototype, really pushed us to design a product or service for the betterment of the lives of modern blacksmiths in the New England area. In a team of 5, we conducted research by visiting blacksmith shops and forges in New England, and held interviews in order to boil down the blacksmiths we met into three all-encompassing imaginary identities known as Personas. From those Personas we identified seven Areas of Opportunity that represented the pain-points that blacksmiths encounter on a day-to-day basis. Finally, after generating and eventually narrowing down hundreds of raw product and service ideas, the Gauntlet was born.

The Gauntlet is essentially an extremely heat-resistant and temperature-responsive glove that assists in the teaching of blacksmithing through an interactive and instructive digital interface. We realized, through our extensive interactions with blacksmiths, that a few of the main pain points were 1) student retention was difficult due to a steep learning curve, 2) inexperienced learners were unsafe in the environment, and 3) experienced smiths desired more precise feedback for temperature-sensitive processes. The Gauntlet addresses all of these pain points, and enhances the blacksmithing experience for blacksmiths of all ages.

You can check out the (Disclaimer: extremely silly) process slides here, the final product poster here, and the final broad interaction map here.

Concentrated Solar Power Generator

Design of Energy Systems | Spring 2014

Design of Energy Systems was a renewable energy-focused upper-level design course for which we learned to break down a system into its components and design and conduct experiments on those components to optimize the system for some objective function.

Throughout the year, we conducted experiments on the individual components of a Concentrated Solar Power Generator system, including a parabolic solar concentrator, a tesla turbine, and a disk generator, in order to determine the overall power and cost efficiencies for the system and systems similar to it. These experiments culminated in a final paper in which we discussed our objective function, the successes and flaws in our system, and future work for the CSP generator. Though our final system did not work as we had expected, improving upon previous work on the CSP generator, fitting the system to work with a tesla turbine, and creating and optimizing a cost-efficiency objective function were all incredibly valuable learning experiences and provided an interesting first step towards a future in renewable energy. (Also, as far as we know, we were the first group of people to ever experiment with using a tesla turbine in a system like this. The common practice is to use a back-driven automobile scroll compressor.)

Electricity Grows in Beakers: Exploring Piezoelectric Crystal Synthesis

Introduction to Materials Science | Project Manager | Fall 2013

The third project of my Intro to MatSci class was to synthesize and confirm the composition and piezoelectric properties of Rochelle Salt (potassium sodium tartrate tetrahydrate). By titrating sodium carbonate into a suspension of potassium bitartrate and precipitating the resulting salt, we were able to create small crystals which we then seeded to grow larger and larger crystals.

We then designed and built a compressive test rig that created an electrical contact with the crystals and displayed the characteristic voltage spikes that resulted from mechanically deforming them. We then further verified the composition of the crystals by performing XRD tests on them to determine the crystal structure. The presentation poster can be found here.

Project Macaron

Summer explorations of macaron-baking techniques | Summer 2013

Being at Olin is weird for me because I am so unlike many "typical" Oliners. Many people here were born knowing they wanted to be engineers while I, as I mentioned earlier, went through much of my college applications process truly believing I would spend the next few years at the Culinary Institute of America. My passions had always lied in a more culinary realm, and almost all of my best (or at least strongest) memories had me in the kitchen trying out a new cake, attempting my grandma's famous cream puff recipe, or whipping up a fancy dinner for my family. I had never really seen myself studying Sustainable Design, and barely knew what that even meant until well into my Olin career. Though school generally keeps me too busy to really cook and continue to seriously pursue my love of food, I still do manage to make a little trouble in the kitchen while I'm home.

Around the time that the cupcake fad began to die down, I discovered French macarons, the most beautiful, delicious, and texturally-intriguing dessert EVER, in a great little bakery near my house. After discovering them, it only took me 2 weeks to try every single one (of the 20) flavors the bakery had to offer, and I knew I had to try to make them myself.

Macarons are a very technically-difficult cookie to bake, and there are many aspects a baker has to get right in order to truly call it a macaron. The cookie has to have a flawlessly-smooth, crispy shell, a chewy yet airy center, a flat bottom, and most importantly, a tall "foot." This perfection was what I was aiming for. Now, every baker has a few failures, but LET ME TELL YOU, my first-pass macarons were SO UGLY. Just awful. And I was discouraged, but I continued to watch the pros make them in YouTube videos, and read blogs of bakers who were paid to test these sorts of recipes out. It took me somewhere around 4 batches to finally get them right (foot and all), and it felt great to finally see my hours come to fruition. It was like the ultimate validation that, though I now call myself an engineer, I've still got a bit of baker in me.

Pickles and Jams

Co-Curricular | Fall 2014

Almost every semester one of our most-loved professors, Jean Huang, teaches a food-centric Co-Curricular (Co-Curriculars are basically fun, usually unrelated to engineering 1-credit "classes"). Pottery

Pottery Passionate Pursuit | Spring 2013 and Fall 2013

In the middle of my freshman year at Olin I learned that there was a pretty sweet (Olin-student run, weirdly enough) ceramics studio at Babson (the business school 5 minutes away), and that Olin students could (and were encouraged to) take classes to learn how to throw pottery. IndiBobo

Not-Entirely-Legitimate-but-Totally-Awesome Dorm Room Business | Fall 2013 and Spring 2014

Olin, notorious for poorly-designed buildings, has one amazing thing to offer in the Freshman-Sophomore dorm and it is HIGH-CEILINGED ROOMS. When my roommate and I won one in the cutthroat bloodbath that is Olin Room Draw, we decided to give back to the school and open up a shop, affectionately known as IndiBobo, where we would serve hot, homemade Indian curry and ice-cold boba milktea (called bubble tea on the East Coast) on Friday nights.

When we first came up with the idea, we had very little clue as to how it would be received by our class (and the school), but on opening night, WE. WERE. SWAMPED. Turns out people at Olin love them a good steaming bowl of curry, paired with some milktea. Though extremely unofficial, our little business grew in popularity and by the end of the year we ended up with a bunch of regulars who depended on us for their late-night snack.
